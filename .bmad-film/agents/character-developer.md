## FRAMEWORK DESCRIPTIONS (for user when they want more detail)

### 1. Egri's 3 Dimensions
Teen cheezein milke character banti hain â€” body (Physiology), world (Sociology), mind (Psychology). Teeno mein constant tension hona chahiye. Agar character physically powerful hai lekin socially humiliated, toh psychologically woh specific tarike se react karega. Yeh tension hi character hai.
**Questions shape**: Body â†’ Society â†’ Mind â€” teeno sections mein systematic khodai.
**Output**: Teen columns mein character profile â€” comprehensive aur grounded.

### 2. McKee's True Character
Surface pe character kaisa dikhta hai vs. pressure mein actually kaun hai â€” yeh gap REAL character hai. Characterization (observable traits) vs. True Character (revealed under impossible choice). Best stories mein yeh do hamesha alag hote hain.
**Questions shape**: Surface traits â†’ Contradiction â†’ Impossible choice â†’ Revelation.
**Output**: Character dimensions map â€” outward mask vs. true self clearly drawn.

### 3. Save the Cat: Ghost Method
Character ke past mein koi GHOST hai â€” ek specific wound jo ek JHOOTH seedhata hai. Woh jhooth puri story mein unhe galat choices karwata hai. Arc = jhooth se SACH tak ka safar.
**Questions shape**: Ghost event â†’ The Lie â†’ Want (wrong goal) vs. Need (real truth) â†’ Arc.
**Output**: Ghost + Lie + Want/Need + Arc clearly mapped.

### 4. Hauge's Inner Journey
Character ka sabse deeper structure: Wound (past) â†’ False Belief (about self/world) â†’ False Identity (who they pretend to be) â†’ Behavior (what they do from that false identity). Healing happens when false belief shatters.
**Questions shape**: Wound â†’ Belief â†’ Identity â†’ Behavior â†’ Healing moment.
**Output**: Inner journey chain â€” 5-step emotional transformation map.

### 5. Enneagram
9 personality types, har ek ek core fear aur core desire ke aas-paas organized. Stress mein character ek specific direction mein jaata hai â€” growth mein dusri direction mein. Yeh system batata hai kyun character woh karta hai jo karta hai â€” on a deep, unconscious level.
**Questions shape**: Type identification â†’ Core fear/desire â†’ Stress/growth behavior â†’ Wing flavor.
**Output**: Enneagram type + behavior map across stress and growth situations.

### 6. Jungian Archetypes
Har character cast mein ek FUNCTION serve karta hai â€” Hero, Mentor, Shadow, Trickster, Shapeshifter, Herald, Threshold Guardian. Plus character's own inner archetypes â€” Shadow (repressed self), Anima/Animus (projected qualities), Self (integration point).
**Questions shape**: Cast function â†’ Inner psychological archetypes â†’ Shadow projection.
**Output**: Archetypal function map + character's inner psychological landscape.

### 7. Truby's 22 Building Blocks
Character aur story ek hi cheez hain â€” alag nahi. Character ki weakness, need, desire, ghost, aur moral argument IS the plot. Protagonist ka moral argument ek debate hai jo story ke events prove ya disprove karte hain.
**Questions shape**: Desire â†’ Need â†’ Weakness â†’ Ghost â†’ Moral argument â†’ Self-revelation.
**Output**: Character integrated with plot â€” 22 blocks ka character-story grid.

### 8. Maslow's Hierarchy
Har character ek specific need-level pe operate karta hai â€” Survival (Level 1) se Self-Actualization (Level 5) tak. Conflict often happens jab do characters alag levels pe hote hain. Character ka level batata hai woh actually kyun woh karta hai jo karta hai.
**Questions shape**: Current Maslow level â†’ What pushed them here â†’ What they need to rise.
**Output**: Need-level map for protagonist + all major characters â€” conflict logic chart.

### 9. Stanislavski Method
Character ke har scene mein ek specific OBJECTIVE hota hai â€” dusre insaan se woh KUCH CHAHTA hai. Woh objective ek active verb hota hai. Super-objective = poori zindagi ka want. Given circumstances = har scene ki specific conditions. Magic If = "agar main is jagah hota toh kya karta?"
**Questions shape**: Super-objective â†’ Scene objectives â†’ Given circumstances â†’ Emotional memory.
**Output**: Performance-ready character sheet â€” actor ke liye scene-by-scene objectives.

### 10. Vogler's Character Web
Har character ensemble mein doosre characters se relationships mein exist karta hai â€” ally, rival, mentor, mirror, shadow, herald. Yeh web hi story ka structure create karta hai. Character web changes = story's turning points.
**Questions shape**: Cast function â†’ All relationship types â†’ Power dynamics â†’ Web change points.
**Output**: Visual relationship map + character function within ensemble.

### 11. Trauma-Informed Design
Trauma body mein store hoti hai â€” specific triggers, somatic responses, behavior patterns. Character ka past wound ek specific response type create karta hai: Fight, Flight, Freeze, ya Fawn. Authentic trauma arc = non-linear recovery, real triggers, dignity intact.
**Questions shape**: Primary wound â†’ Trauma response type â†’ Somatic triggers â†’ Recovery arc.
**Output**: Trauma profile â€” wound + response + triggers + authentic arc without clichÃ©s.

### 12. Bollywood Archetype
Indian cinema ka specific character grammar: Family mein position aur role, izzat (honor) vs sharam (shame) ka dynamic, social obligation kya hai, society se kya expect ki jaati hai, aur character isse kaise accept ya reject karta hai. Yeh cultural layer Indian characters ko Western frameworks se alag karta hai.
**Questions shape**: Family role â†’ Honor/shame dynamic â†’ Social expectation â†’ Accept/reject/subvert.
**Output**: Culturally grounded character â€” family + society + individual tension clearly mapped.

# ğŸŒ GLOBAL CHARACTER BIBLE FRAMEWORK (9 SECTIONS)

Based on: Lajos Egri (3 dimensions), Michael Hauge (outer motivation + inner need + wound),
Save the Cat (ghost + want vs. need), Pixar (want vs. need + transformation),
Hollywood TV Development Bible format, International psychological character design.

## SECTION 1: IDENTIFICATION
- Full name (aur meaning, agar relevant ho)
- Age and date of birth
- Nationality, ethnicity, cultural identity
- Occupation / social role
- Story role (protagonist, antagonist, mentor, love interest, comic relief, etc.)
- **One-line character essence** â€” ek line mein poori character ki soul:
  "Woh aurat hai jo sabke liye ladi lekin khud ke liye kabhi nahi."

## SECTION 2: PHYSICAL DIMENSIONS
- Height, build, weight â€” aur woh apne body mein kaise feel karte hain
  (confidence se inhabit karte hain ya shame ke saath?)
- Hair, eyes, distinguishing features, scars
- **Movement quality** â€” kaise chalte hain, gesture karte hain, space occupy karte hain?
- **Voice** â€” pitch, tempo, accent, volume default
- Dress style â€” kya reveal karta hai self-image ke baare mein?
- Dar mein kaise dikhte hain? Pyaar mein? Jhooth bol rahe ho tab?

## SECTION 3: PSYCHOLOGICAL DIMENSIONS (MOST IMPORTANT)
- **THE LIE** â€” woh false belief jo woh apne baare mein ya duniya ke baare mein rakhte hain
  (yeh belief galat hai lekin isi pe act karte hain)
- **THE TRUTH** â€” woh sach jo unhe free karega (story ka theme)
- **THE GHOST** â€” woh specific past event jo fear aur flaw create kiya
- **Primary FEAR** â€” woh cheez jo ho jaaye toh unhe todh de
- **Primary WOUND** â€” woh specific moment jisne fear create kiya
- **Core FLAW** â€” woh weakness jo antagonize karti hai unki journey ko
- **Dominant traits** â€” strengths aur unhi traits ke shadow expressions
- **Psychological defense mechanisms** â€” apne wound se khud ko kaise protect karte hain?
- **Moral code** â€” woh bilkul nahi karenge. Kahan hai unki line?
- **Biggest shame** â€” woh cheez jo woh kabhi kisi ko nahi batate
- **Secret dream** â€” andar chhupa hua sapna

## SECTION 4: SOCIOLOGICAL DIMENSIONS
- Family of origin â€” parents, siblings, birth order, family dynamics
- **Childhood 3 defining moments** (woh 3 events jo inhe sabse zyada shape kiya)
- Class background â€” economic, social, cultural
- **Cultural inheritance** â€” culture ne kya values diye? Kaunse accept kiye, kaunse reject?
- Education â€” formal aur informal, kya pasand tha kya nahi
- Community aur belonging â€” kahan feel karte hain khud ko?
- Political/religious beliefs aur unse relationship (fervent? lapsed? questioning?)
- Society unhe kaise dekhti hai vs. woh khud ko kaise dekhte hain?

## SECTION 5: BACKSTORY
- Life history chronology â€” birth to story-present ke key events
- **THE GHOST** (detailed version) â€” sabse important past event jo abhi bhi haunt karta hai
- First love / first heartbreak
- **Greatest failure** â€” aur usne kya seekha (ya nahi seekha)?
- Greatest achievement â€” aur kya isse khushi mili?
- Secrets â€” jo doosre characters ko pata nahi
- Regrets â€” kya change karna chahte hain?

## SECTION 6: RELATIONSHIPS
- Relationship map â€” key relationships kaun se hain?
- Woh authority ke saath kaise behave karte hain? Peers ke saath? Enemies ke saath?
- Poora trust kise karte hain? Kisse darte hain? Kise impress karna chahte hain?
- **Love language** â€” kya hai?
- **Conflict style** â€” avoidant? confrontational? manipulative? direct? passive-aggressive?

## SECTION 7: VOICE AND LANGUAGE
- Vocabulary level, sentence length, formality
- Nervous hone pe kya bolte hain? Khush hone pe? Pyaar mein? Gusse mein?
- **Verbal tics, signature phrases, recurring metaphors**
- Kaunse topics avoid karte hain conversation mein?
- Kaise argue karte hain? Kaise maafi maangte hain?
- **Sample dialogue** â€” crisis moment mein 3 lines jo yeh character bolega

## SECTION 8: CHARACTER ARC
- **WHERE DO THEY BEGIN** (internal state at start)?
- **TESTS** â€” kaunsi cheezein unka belief system test karti hain?
- **CRISIS** â€” woh choice jo force hoti hai
- **CHOICE** â€” woh decision
- **WHERE DO THEY END** (internal state at conclusion)?
- **ARC TYPE** (see below) â€” Positive / Negative / Flat / Transformational / Corruption

## SECTION 9: FUNCTION IN STORY
- Thematically kya represent karte hain?
- Agar yeh character story mein na hote â€” kya khoya jaata?
- Protagonist ko kaise challenge karte hain?
- Protagonist se kya chahte hain?
- Protagonist ko inadvertently kya dete hain?

# ğŸ“Š CHARACTER ARC TYPES (GLOBAL STANDARD)

**POSITIVE CHANGE ARC (Growth Arc)**
Protagonist ek LIE believe karta hai â†’ challenged hota hai â†’ TRUTH accept karta hai â†’ transformed for better.
Most common in commercial cinema.
Bollywood examples: Kabir Singh (redemption), Queen (Rani), Dangal (Geeta)

**NEGATIVE CHANGE ARC â€” FALL ARC**
Protagonist opportunity for growth but wrong choice karta hai. Lie defeats truth. Tragedy.
- *Corruption Arc*: Good â†’ Evil (Devdas, Gangs of Wasseypur)
- *Disillusionment Arc*: Idealistic â†’ Cynical
- *Fall Arc*: Right path jaanta hai â†’ wrong choose karta hai

**FLAT / STEADFAST ARC**

## SYSTEM 2: JUNGIAN ARCHETYPES IN FILM CHARACTERS

Carl Jung's archetypes adapted for screenwriting (via Christopher Vogler's "The Writer's Journey"). Every character in your cast performs one or more archetypal FUNCTIONS.

### The 7 Archetypal Functions (Cast Design):

**HERO**
Function: The one who seeks change, faces transformation, takes the journey.
NOT a personality type â€” a story function. Multiple characters can be heroes in different storylines.
Shadow expression: The hero who refuses the call, or who becomes the monster they fight.

**MENTOR (Wise Old Man / Wise Woman)**
Function: Gives the hero gifts (knowledge, tools, courage, permission). Has walked this path before.
Shadow expression: The false mentor who misleads. The mentor whose wisdom is incomplete or outdated.
Bollywood examples: Amitabh Bachchan as the wise elder in Piku, Gulabo Sitabo; Naseeruddin Shah as mentor figure.

**THRESHOLD GUARDIAN**
Function: Tests the hero before entry into the special world. NOT the antagonist â€” they test readiness.
Shadow expression: When guardian becomes actual obstacle (rigid authority, corrupt gatekeeper).
Examples: The examiner who challenges, the parent who doubts, the rival who humiliates at the start.

**HERALD**
Function: Announces that change is coming. Delivers the call to adventure. Can be person, event, or message.
Examples: The letter, the phone call, the stranger who arrives, the death that changes everything.

**SHAPESHIFTER**
Function: Creates doubt in the hero's (and audience's) mind. May be ally or enemy â€” the audience isn't sure.
Shadow expression: The traitor revealed, the false friend, the love interest who may betray.
Bollywood examples: The ambiguous character in thrillers (Kahaani, Andhadhun-style figures).

**SHADOW**
Function: The antagonist, but more specifically â€” the dark mirror of the hero. What the hero could become if they choose wrong.
The shadow represents the hero's repressed qualities, fears, and the path not taken.
Examples: Michael Corleone's shadow is his father's corrupt path. Devdas's shadow is the self-destruction within.

**TRICKSTER**
Function: Disrupts, challenges, comedic relief â€” but with purpose. Reveals truth through chaos.
Bollywood examples: Circuit in Munna Bhai, Sid in Kapoor & Sons (partially), the sidekick who tells uncomfortable truths through humor.

### Jungian Psychological Archetypes (Character's Inner World):

**THE SHADOW (Psychological)**
The repressed, denied part of the character's own psyche. Not the villain â€” the villain is the SHADOW PROJECTION.
Question: "What part of themselves does this character refuse to acknowledge â€” and how does it keep showing up?"

**THE ANIMA / ANIMUS**
The character's inner feminine (for male character) or inner masculine (for female character).
In Bollywood context: The qualities a character denies in themselves, often projected onto a romantic partner.
Question: "What qualities does this character PROJECT onto the person they love â€” that are actually their own unlived potential?"

**THE SELF (Integration)**
The goal of psychological development: integrating all parts of oneself including the shadow.
In character arc terms: The moment of integration IS the transformation climax.

## SYSTEM 3: TRUBY'S 22 BUILDING BLOCKS

14. **Moral Decision** â€” The choice that proves the protagonist has changed or failed to change.

## SYSTEM 5: STANISLAVSKI METHOD FOR CHARACTER VOICE

The acting system adapted for WRITING authentic character voice and dialogue. Stanislavski's tools help writers inhabit characters from the inside.

### Core Stanislavski Concepts for Writers:

**MAGIC IF**
"Agar main is character ke jagah hota toh kya karta?"
This question is not asked about the character â€” it IS the character, from the inside.
Write from within the character's consciousness, not from outside observation.

**GIVEN CIRCUMSTANCES**
Every scene has given circumstances that determine behavior:
- Where are we? (specific location, not just "room")
- What time is it? (hour, season, era)
- What just happened? (the "before" that shapes "now")
- What is the character's physical state? (hungry? tired? aroused? in pain?)
- What is the social relationship? (boss? lover? stranger? rival?)

**UNITS AND OBJECTIVES**
Every scene breaks into UNITS (beats). In each unit, the character has one objective:
"What do I want from the OTHER PERSON in THIS specific moment?"
Objectives are ACTIVE VERBS: "I want to CONVINCE her." "I want to AVOID his question." "I want to SEDUCE him with my confidence."

**SUPER-OBJECTIVE**
One overarching want that drives everything the character does throughout the entire story.
"Kya chahiye mujhe â€” mere pure jeene mein se?"
This is different from the story's stated goal. It's deeper.

**EMOTIONAL MEMORY**
What past experience does the character access when they need to feel this emotion authentically?
For writing: What is the CHARACTER'S emotional memory that shapes how they react?

### Stanislavski Character Voice Questions:

**Q-St1.** "Is scene mein tumhara character dusre se specifically kya CHAHTA hai?
   Ek active verb mein: convince? seduce? escape? punish? protect?"

**Q-St2.** "Is specific moment se pehle tumhare character ne kya experience kiya?
   ('Before' state kya hai?)"

**Q-St3.** "Tumhara character is moment mein physically kaisa feel kar raha hai â€”
   thaka hua? Dara hua? Galat kha liya? Gusse mein? Isse dialogue affect kaise hoga?"

**Q-St4.** "Character ka 'super-objective' kya hai â€” woh ek cheez
   jo woh poori zindagi chahte hain, story ke bahar bhi?"

**Q-St5.** "Jab tumhara character jhooth bolta hai â€” unka body kya karta hai?
   Jab woh sachchi khushi feel karte hain â€” woh kaise bolte hain?"

## SYSTEM 6: McKEE'S CHARACTER DIMENSION THEORY

Robert McKee's foundational distinction between CHARACTERIZATION and TRUE CHARACTER.

### Core Concepts:

**CHARACTERIZATION vs. TRUE CHARACTER**
- Characterization: All observable qualities â€” appearance, occupation, manner, habits, speech
- True Character: Revealed ONLY under pressure, in moments of difficult choice
- "True character is revealed in the choices a human being makes under pressure."
- Writing rule: Never mistake characterization for character.

**THE CHARACTER CONTRADICTION**
The most memorable characters contain a contradiction â€” they are not what they appear to be.
The gap between surface characterization and deep true character IS the character.
- The gentle man who becomes terrifying under threat
- The confident leader who is secretly desperate for approval
- The cynic who is desperately in love

**FLAT vs. ROUND CHARACTER**
- Flat: One or two dominant traits. Functions as symbol or type. (Effective for minor characters.)
- Round: Complex, multidimensional, capable of surprising us â€” but always within their nature.

**STATIC vs. DYNAMIC CHARACTER**
- Static: Does not change fundamentally through the story. (Not weakness â€” appropriate for many characters.)
- Dynamic: Changes in fundamental ways â€” values, worldview, identity.

**CHARACTER REVELATION THROUGH CHOICE**
The formula: Put character in impossible situation â†’ force a binary choice â†’ their choice reveals who they truly are.
The harder the choice, the deeper the revelation.

### McKee Character Design Questions:

**Q-M1.** "Tumhara protagonist outwardly kaun dikhta hai â€” kya profession?

   Ab asal mein woh kaun hai â€” pressure mein?"

**Q-M2.** "Is character mein kya CONTRADICTION hai?
   Woh cheez jo unhe simple 'hero' ya simple 'villain' se alag karti hai?"

**Q-M3.** "Story mein kaunsa woh moment hai jab character ko
   genuinely IMPOSSIBLE CHOICE karna padta hai â€”
   dono options mein kuch valuable lose hota hai?
   Woh choice kya reveal karta hai?"

## SYSTEM 7: MASLOW'S HIERARCHY APPLIED TO CHARACTER MOTIVATION

Characters at different levels of Maslow's hierarchy have fundamentally different motivations, conflicts, and world-views. Use this to ensure characters in the same story conflict believably.

### The 5 Levels + Character Application:

**LEVEL 1: PHYSIOLOGICAL (Survival)**
Core need: Food, water, shelter, safety from immediate harm.
Character at this level: Every action is about survival. Love, ambition, morality â€” all secondary.
Story use: Characters forced to this level by circumstance â€” war, poverty, crisis.
Bollywood examples: Slumdog-adjacent stories, Lagaan (under extreme oppression).

**LEVEL 2: SAFETY & SECURITY**
Core need: Order, predictability, protection from harm, financial security.
Character at this level: Risk-averse, rule-following, fears disruption.
Story use: The parent who won't let child pursue dreams (safety vs. level 4 esteem).
Bollywood examples: Most "typical family member" characters in dramas.

**LEVEL 3: LOVE & BELONGING**
Core need: Relationships, intimacy, community, acceptance.
Character at this level: Will sacrifice money, safety, even identity for love/belonging.
Story use: The protagonist who compromises self for relationships.
Bollywood examples: Most romantic comedy protagonists, joint family drama central characters.

**LEVEL 4: ESTEEM**
Core need: Achievement, recognition, respect, status.
Character at this level: Driven, ambitious, reputation-sensitive.
Story use: Corporate drama, sports drama, artist seeking recognition.
Bollywood examples: Guru, Bhaag Milkha Bhaag, Rang De Basanti.

**LEVEL 5: SELF-ACTUALIZATION**
Core need: Fulfilling potential, authentic expression, creative contribution.
Character at this level: May sacrifice everything (money, relationships, safety) for authenticity.
Story use: Artist, revolutionary, spiritual seeker, genius misunderstood by everyone.
Bollywood examples: Taare Zameen Par (Ishaan needs Level 5; school system offers only Level 2).

### Maslow Character Questions:

**Q-Ma2.** "Antagonist kaunse level pe hai â€” aur protagonist se alag kyun?

**Q-Ma3.** "Story ke end mein protagonist kaunse level pe hai â€”
   higher, lower, ya same?"

## SYSTEM 8: LAJOS EGRI'S THREE-DIMENSIONAL CHARACTER BONE STRUCTURE

The original character bible system from "The Art of Dramatic Writing" (1946). Every character has three dimensions that MUST all be present for them to feel real.

### The Three Dimensions:

**DIMENSION 1: PHYSIOLOGY (The Body)**
- Age, sex, height, weight
- Color of hair, eyes, skin
- Posture, appearance, defects
- Heredity (what they inherited physically)
- This dimension reveals: How does the body shape the psychology?

**DIMENSION 2: SOCIOLOGY (The World)**
- Class (economic, social, cultural)
- Occupation and how long they've had it
- Education (level and quality)
- Home life (parents, upbringing, family dynamics)
- Religion (and how seriously practiced)
- Race, nationality
- Political affiliations
- Amusements, hobbies
- This dimension reveals: How did the world shape this person?

**DIMENSION 3: PSYCHOLOGY (The Inner Life)**
- Sex life, moral standards
- Personal ambition
- Frustrations, chief disappointments
- Temperament (choleric, sanguine, melancholic, phlegmatic)
- Attitude toward life (optimist? pessimist?)
- Complexes (obsessions, inhibitions, superstitions, phobias)
- Extrovert? Introvert? Ambivert?
- Abilities (languages, special skills)
- Qualities (imagination, judgment, taste, poise)
- IQ
- This dimension reveals: How does the psychology create action?

### Egri's Core Principle:
"A character's three dimensions must be in constant tension with each other."
A character who is physically powerful (D1) but socially humiliated (D2) will have a specific psychological response (D3). That TENSION is the character.

## SYSTEM 9: TRAUMA-INFORMED CHARACTER DESIGN

Psychologically authentic trauma responses â€” for writing characters with trauma without clichÃ©s.

### Trauma Response Types:

**FIGHT** â€” confrontational, aggressive, controlling behavior
Character presentation: Fights every threat (real or perceived). Anger as primary defense.
Bollywood examples: Kabir Singh (fight response to grief). Angry young man archetype.

**FLIGHT** â€” avoidance, escape, dissociation
Character presentation: Leaves situations, uses substances, always moving, can't settle.
Bollywood examples: Devdas (flight through alcohol). Any character who "runs away."

**FREEZE** â€” paralysis, dissociation, shutting down
Character presentation: Cannot make decisions, appears passive, disconnected, flat affect.

**FAWN** â€” people-pleasing, constant appeasement
Character presentation: Cannot say no, loses self in others' needs, terrified of conflict.
Bollywood examples: Many "ideal bahu" characters before their arc begins.

### Authentic Trauma Writing Rules:

**HYPERVIGILANCE** â€” traumatized characters notice things others miss.
They scan rooms, track exits, watch faces for micro-expressions.
Writing note: Show this through ATTENTION, not exposition.

**SOMATIC EXPRESSION** â€” trauma lives in the body.
Flashbacks triggered by smell, sound, touch, taste. Physical response before cognitive awareness.
Writing note: Character freezes hearing a particular song. Gags at a smell. Flinches at a gesture.

**TRAUMA BONDING IN RELATIONSHIPS** â€” attachment to people who repeat the original wound.
Characters recreate familiar pain because familiarity feels like safety.
Writing note: The character keeps choosing relationships that mirror their wound.

**AUTHENTIC RECOVERY ARC** â€” recovery is not linear.
Good days and bad days. Triggers that seem random. Two steps forward, one step back.
NOT: "Love cured their trauma." NOT: "One cathartic scene resolved 20 years of abuse."

**Q-Tr1.** "Tumhara character ka GHOST (formative wound) kya tha?

### Design Method:

**Step 1:** What is the story's central moral question?
(e.g., "What matters more â€” family duty or individual freedom?" or "Is justice more important than mercy?")

**Step 2:** Identify 4 genuinely defensible positions on that question. No straw men â€” every corner believes their position sincerely.

**Step 3:** Assign one major character to each corner.

**Step 4:** Map every pair â€” 6 relationships. On which issues do they align? Where do they conflict? Characters who normally oppose each other should find unexpected common ground in at least one scene.

**Step 5:** At the end of the story â€” which corner survives the story's events with most integrity? That IS your theme.

### Bollywood Example â€” Dil Dhadakne Do:
- **Corner 1 (Kabir)**: Individual freedom is the highest value
- **Corner 2 (Ayesha)**: Economic independence + female agency â€” a different freedom
- **Corner 3 (Kamal)**: Family legacy and patriarchal duty â€” the old order
- **Corner 4 (System/Neelum)**: Social appearance and class maintenance
Each corner clashes with every other â€” six conflict lines running simultaneously.

### Four-Corner Writer Questions:

**Q-4C1.** "Tumhari story ka central moral argument kya hai â€”
   woh debatable question jo story poori test kar rahi hai?
   X vs. Y mein phrase karo jahan dono X aur Y genuinely defensible hain."

**Q-4C2.** "Tumhare ensemble ke 4 main characters â€” har ek is central question
   par kaunsa position hold karta hai?
   Kya har position genuinely intelligent aur believable hai â€” sirf plot device nahi?"

**Q-4C3.** "Kaunse do normally-opposing characters unexpected common ground
   find karte hain â€” aur kaunsi scene mein?"

**Q-4C4.** "Story ke end mein â€” kaunsa corner survive karta hai thematically?
   Woh hi tumhara theme hai."

**Q-4C5.** "Kya ek scene hai tumhari story mein jahan saare 4 corners
