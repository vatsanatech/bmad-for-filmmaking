## FRAMEWORK DESCRIPTIONS (jab director/DP zyada detail chahe)

### 1. Classical Coverage
Hollywood ka tried-and-tested standard. Master shot (wide) ‚Üí Coverage shots (medium + close) ‚Üí Inserts aur cutaways. 180-degree rule strictly followed ‚Äî axis nahi tooti. Editor ke paas enough material hai. Safe, reliable, professional.
**Shot approach**: Master first, then coverage in both directions. Two-person scene = 5 shots minimum (master + 2x OTS + 2x CU). Insert shots for important objects.
**Best For**: Dialogue scenes, ensemble sequences, anything needing edit flexibility. Sabse "safe" choice ‚Äî production standard.

### 2. Auteur Visual Grammar
Director ka consistent personal visual language ‚Äî satyajit Ray ka transparent elegance, Kubrick ka symmetry, Bergman ka extreme close-up on face. Ek recognizable style jo film ke across consistent hai. Pre-visualized frames se start karo.
**Shot approach**: Signature shots identified upfront. Frame compositions planned before shoot. Visual motifs (recurring angles/objects) deliberately built in. Style serves theme.
**Best For**: Jab director ka clear personal aesthetic vision ho. Film festivals, artistic work.

### 3. Bollywood Visual Language
Vibrant primary colors dominate ‚Äî deep ruby reds, electric blues, luminous golds. Dynamic camera tracking + dramatic zooms. Song sequences mein choreography + cinematography ek saath planned. Emotional peaks pe close-up heavy. Grandeur aur spectacle valued.
**Shot approach**: Song sequences separate visual grammar (song-specific storyboard). Pre-climax emotional peak = tight close-ups. Landscape shots ki grandeur. Pan + tilt kaa free use.
**Best For**: Commercial Hindi films, song sequences, emotional entertainers.

### 4. Handheld/Naturalistic (Dogme 95 / Neorealism)
Camera handheld ‚Äî breathes with character. Natural light only (or practical lights). Documentary feel creates intimacy. No composed "pretty shots" ‚Äî reality over aesthetics. Anurag Kashyap, Italian neorealists, Dogme 95.
**Shot approach**: Handheld through entire film (no tripod except when story demands static). Available/natural light. No complex setups. Camera follows character, doesn't lead. Improvised feel.
**Best For**: Crime dramas, urban realism, gritty character stories. Authenticity over beauty.

### 5. Static/Contemplative (Tarkovsky/Slow Cinema)
Long takes where NOTHING MOVES ‚Äî yet everything happens. Time itself becomes subject. Stasis creates contemplation. Static wide shots. Audience must actively watch, not passively receive. Spiritual, meditative quality.
**Shot approach**: Static setups held long after "action" completes. Camera on tripod, no movement. Characters enter/exit frame ‚Äî camera stays. Minimal cutting. Long takes as discipline.
**Best For**: Art cinema, grief stories, films about time and memory. Myra-type emotional landscapes.

### 6. Genre-Coded Visual
**THRILLER**: Tight frames (claustrophobia). High contrast lighting (Noir shadows). Dutch angles (disorientation). Deep focus (every corner suspect). Fast cutting tension builds.
**ROMANCE**: Soft lighting, warm palette. Two-shots (connection). Wide shots (world as character). Smooth dollies and cranes (flowing emotion). Shallow depth of field (isolate lovers).
**HORROR**: Low-key lighting (darkness hides). Dutch angles + low angles (unease). Static wide (dread building) then sudden close (jump). POV shots (victim immersion).
**COMEDY**: High-key bright lighting (clarity). Medium shots (reaction visible). Timing-based cuts (comedy rhythm). No distracting compositions.
**Shot approach**: Genre conventions as starting rules ‚Äî subvert deliberately when needed, not accidentally.

### 7. Color/Light Driven
Palette is a character. Color tells story before dialogue does. Lighting reveals psychology. Breaking Bad mein Walter White's color shifts with his moral descent. Am√©lie mein green = hope + red = passion. Every major scene has intentional color intent.
**Shot approach**: Color script designed before shooting (what palette = what emotion). Key light sources as narrative devices. Shadows cast intentionally. Color shifts signal character arc changes.
**Best For**: Films where visual metaphor must carry emotional weight. Strong thematic color coding.

### 8. Montage-Based (Eisenstein/Kuleshov Tradition)
CUT = MEANING. Two shots together create a third idea neither image alone has. Kuleshov Effect ‚Äî audience creates meaning from juxtaposition. Editing rhythm IS the storytelling. Soviet montage theories applied.
**Shot approach**: Individual shots designed for their montage relationship, not standalone. Editing rhythm planned in shot breakdown. Shot A + Shot B = Idea C built into planning. Music-picture relationship carefully designed.
**Best For**: Films where editing is primary artistic tool. Montage sequences as meaning-making.

### 9. Mise-en-Sc√®ne (Bazin's Aesthetic)
Staging over cutting. What happens IN the frame matters more than how frames are assembled. Deep focus (background as clear as foreground). Characters' position in frame communicates power/relationship. Long takes with in-scene movement. Camera moves to follow staging, not to create meaning through cuts.
**Shot approach**: Pre-block scenes carefully. Camera placement captures choreography. Multiple characters in frame simultaneously (no cutting between). Deep focus preferred. Character blocking IS the visual grammar.
**Best For**: Theatre-influenced films, intimate ensemble dramas, films valuing continuity of performance.

### 10. Single-Take Sequence (Oner)
Ek continuous shot ‚Äî koi cut nahi. Real-time aur immersive. Audience can't breathe ‚Äî camera doesn't either. Characters, camera, and story move together in one uninterrupted take. Complex choreography between actors, camera, and space.
**Shot approach**: Map entire sequence before shoot (camera path + actor blocking). Multiple rehearsals essential. Every department (lighting, sound, props) synchronized. Long single takes planned for maximum dramatic moment.
**Best For**: Key climactic sequences, chase scenes, discovery moments, intimate confrontations. Sparingly but powerfully used.

### 11. Symbolic/Poetic Visual (Visual Metaphor Language)
Objects carry story weight ‚Äî scarf, stone, window, door. Each recurring object is a symbolic unit. Visual metaphors built into shot design. Framing itself is metaphorical (character trapped by frame = trapped by situation). Composition creates meaning beyond narrative.
**Shot approach**: Symbolic object inventory created first. Key recurring visual motifs identified. Insert shots of objects designed as metaphorical statements. Framing choices encode thematic meaning.
**Best For**: Lyrical dramas, art films. PERFECT FOR MYRA ‚Äî scarf, stones, branches, dog's absence = visual language.

### 12. Economical Coverage
Budget constraint = creative constraint = creative strength. Minimal setups ‚Äî maximum story. One location, strategic angles. Natural light planning (golden hour, overcast, etc.). Pre-visualize obsessively ‚Äî no exploration on set. Every shot decision costs money.
**Shot approach**: Shot count target set upfront. Locations chosen for built-in visual interest (no dressing needed). Master shot + 1 coverage angle max per scene. Natural light schedule planned. Zero waste.
**Best For**: Low-budget films, first-time directors, 1-2 day shoots, minimal equipment.

## Genre-Specific Visual Languages

### **THRILLER** (Anurag Kashyap / Sriram Raghavan style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Close-ups and tight frames (suffocation, paranoia)
- High/low angles (power dynamics)
- Deep focus (everything visible = everything suspicious)
- Static frames then sudden movement (tension building)
- Noir lighting (harsh shadows, underlit faces)

**Shot Choices**:
- CLOSE UP for lies, secrets, concealment
- WIDE SHOT HIGH ANGLE for vulnerability
- LOW ANGLE for menace
- DUTCH ANGLE for disorientation
- SLOW PUSH IN for rising tension

**Transitions**: CUT TO (abrupt), SMASH CUT TO (shock), MATCH CUT

---

### **ROMANCE** (Karan Johar / Imtiaz Ali style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Soft focus and warm lighting (intimacy, emotion)
- Medium shots and two-shots (connection)
- Smooth camera moves (flowing emotion)
- Natural light (authenticity)
- Depth of field (isolate lovers from world)

**Shot Choices**:
- TWO SHOT for connection
- CLOSE UP for emotional beats
- OVER SHOULDER for conversation intimacy
- WIDE SHOT for environment as character
- SLOW DOLLY IN for emotional intensity

**Transitions**: DISSOLVE (dreamlike), CUT TO, FADE TO

---

### **HORROR** (Ram Gopal Varma style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Low angles and dutch angles (unease)
- Deep shadows (dread)
- Static wide shots then sudden movement (jump scares)
- POV shots (victim perspective)
- Handheld for chaos

**Shot Choices**:
- WIDE SHOT STATIC for dread building
- EXTREME CLOSE UP for terror
- LOW ANGLE DUTCH for supernatural
- POV SHOT for victim experience
- WHIP PAN for sudden horror

**Transitions**: HARD CUT (shock), SMASH CUT TO, FADE TO BLACK

### **COMEDY** (Rajkumar Hirani / Priyadarshan style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Bright, even lighting (clarity)
- Medium shots (character reactions visible)

- Clean frames (no distraction from gag)
- Steadicam for energy

**Shot Choices**:
- MEDIUM SHOT for reactions
- TWO SHOT for comedy timing
- CLOSE UP for punchline reactions
- WIDE SHOT for physical comedy
- WHIP PAN for energy

**Transitions**: CUT TO (timing), QUICK CUT (energy), SMASH CUT TO (punchline)

## Shot Types Reference

### **SHOT SIZES**:
- **EXTREME WIDE SHOT (EWS)** - Establish environment, character tiny in frame
- **WIDE SHOT (WS)** - Full body, environment visible
- **MEDIUM WIDE SHOT (MWS)** - Knees up, some environment
- **MEDIUM SHOT (MS)** - Waist up, character prominent
- **MEDIUM CLOSE UP (MCU)** - Chest up, face prominent
- **CLOSE UP (CU)** - Face fills frame, emotion clear
- **EXTREME CLOSE UP (ECU)** - Eyes, hands, details

### **CAMERA ANGLES**:
- **EYE LEVEL** - Neutral, objective
- **HIGH ANGLE** - Looking down, vulnerability, weakness
- **LOW ANGLE** - Looking up, power, menace
- **DUTCH ANGLE** - Tilted, disorientation, unease
- **BIRD'S EYE** - Straight down, god's view
- **WORM'S EYE** - Straight up, towering

### **CAMERA MOVEMENTS**:
- **STATIC** - No movement, stability or tension
- **PAN** - Horizontal rotation
- **TILT** - Vertical rotation
- **DOLLY IN/OUT** - Camera moves toward/away
- **TRACKING SHOT** - Camera follows subject
- **CRANE SHOT** - Camera rises/descends
- **HANDHELD** - Shaky, documentary feel, chaos
- **STEADICAM** - Smooth movement, flowing
- **WHIP PAN** - Fast pan, energy or transition

### **FRAMING**:
- **OVER SHOULDER (OS)** - From behind one character looking at another
- **POINT OF VIEW (POV)** - See what character sees
- **TWO SHOT** - Two characters in frame
- **THREE SHOT** - Three characters in frame
- **INSERT SHOT** - Close up of object/detail
- **CUTAWAY** - Related but separate action

### **SPECIAL SHOTS**:
- **MONTAGE** - Series of quick shots showing progression
- **SPLIT SCREEN** - Multiple images simultaneously
- **MATCH CUT** - Visual similarity bridges two shots
- **REACTION SHOT** - Character reacting to something

## Transitions Reference

- **CUT TO:** - Standard transition (instant)
- **SMASH CUT TO:** - Abrupt, shocking transition
- **MATCH CUT TO:** - Visual similarity bridges scenes
- **JUMP CUT TO:** - Jarring, time skip
- **DISSOLVE TO:** - Gradual blend (time passing, dreamlike)
- **FADE TO BLACK:** - Gradual fade to darkness (ending, time passing)
- **FADE IN:** - Gradual appearance from black (beginning)
- **FADE OUT:** - Gradual to black (ending)
- **WIPE TO:** - One image replaces another (stylized)

**Most Common in Bollywood**:
- CUT TO: (90% of transitions)
- SMASH CUT TO: (for shock/emphasis)
- DISSOLVE TO: (montages, time passing)
- FADE TO BLACK: (end of sequences)

- What's the pace? (Fast cutting or longer takes)
- What's the character journey? (Shot choices support arc)

### **STEP 2: Scene Analysis**
For each scene:
- **Master shot needed?** (Establish geography)
- **Coverage needed?** (Close-ups, reactions, inserts)
- **Special shots?** (POV, insert, montage)
- **Emotional beats?** (Close-ups for emotion)
- **Transitions?** (How does it connect to next scene)

### **STEP 3: Shot Selection**
Choose shots based on:
- **Story purpose**: What does this moment need to convey?
- **Genre conventions**: Thriller = tight frames, romance = soft intimacy
- **Coverage**: Can editor cut this? Enough angles?
- **Production reality**: Is this shootable? Equipment needed?
- **Visual flow**: Does it cut smoothly to next shot?

### **STEP 4: Write Shooting Script**
Format each shot:
```
SHOT TYPE (ANGLE/MOVEMENT) - SUBJECT, action description.
Detailed visual of what we see and how it feels.

                    CHARACTER
                 (delivery note)
          Dialogue in Hindi/Hinglish.

                                                    TRANSITION:
```

### **STEP 5: Coverage Strategy**
Ensure adequate coverage:
- **Master shot** (wide) - Shows geography, all characters
- **Coverage shots** (medium/close) - Reactions, dialogue
- **Insert shots** (extreme close) - Objects, details, hands
- **Reaction shots** - Character responses
- **Cutaways** - B-roll, environment, atmosphere

**Rule**: If you can't edit the scene from these shots, you don't have enough coverage.

## Bollywood Cinematography Standards

### **Shot Duration Guidelines**:
- **Establishing shots**: 3-5 seconds (unless slow reveal)
- **Wide shots**: 4-8 seconds (depending on action)
- **Medium shots**: 3-6 seconds (dialogue, movement)
- **Close-ups**: 2-4 seconds (emotion, reaction)
- **Extreme close-ups**: 1-2 seconds (detail, impact)
- **Montage shots**: 1-3 seconds (quick cutting)

### **Coverage Ratio**:
- **Dialogue scenes**: 1 master + 2 coverage angles minimum
- **Action scenes**: 5+ angles for cutting flexibility
- **Emotional beats**: Multiple close-ups (different angles)
- **Montages**: 10-15 quick shots

### **Visual Continuity**:
Always note:
- **Screen direction**: Character facing left/right (maintain axis)
- **Eyeline**: Character looking at correct spot
- **Props/costumes**: Consistency across shots
- **Lighting**: Matches between shots in same scene
