## Genre-Specific Visual Languages

### **THRILLER** (Anurag Kashyap / Sriram Raghavan style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Close-ups and tight frames (suffocation, paranoia)
- High/low angles (power dynamics)
- Deep focus (everything visible = everything suspicious)
- Static frames then sudden movement (tension building)
- Noir lighting (harsh shadows, underlit faces)

**Shot Choices**:
- CLOSE UP for lies, secrets, concealment
- WIDE SHOT HIGH ANGLE for vulnerability
- LOW ANGLE for menace
- DUTCH ANGLE for disorientation
- SLOW PUSH IN for rising tension

**Transitions**: CUT TO (abrupt), SMASH CUT TO (shock), MATCH CUT

---

### **ROMANCE** (Karan Johar / Imtiaz Ali style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Soft focus and warm lighting (intimacy, emotion)
- Medium shots and two-shots (connection)
- Smooth camera moves (flowing emotion)
- Natural light (authenticity)
- Depth of field (isolate lovers from world)

**Shot Choices**:
- TWO SHOT for connection
- CLOSE UP for emotional beats
- OVER SHOULDER for conversation intimacy
- WIDE SHOT for environment as character
- SLOW DOLLY IN for emotional intensity

**Transitions**: DISSOLVE (dreamlike), CUT TO, FADE TO

---

### **HORROR** (Ram Gopal Varma style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Low angles and dutch angles (unease)
- Deep shadows (dread)
- Static wide shots then sudden movement (jump scares)
- POV shots (victim perspective)
- Handheld for chaos

**Shot Choices**:
- WIDE SHOT STATIC for dread building
- EXTREME CLOSE UP for terror
- LOW ANGLE DUTCH for supernatural
- POV SHOT for victim experience
- WHIP PAN for sudden horror

**Transitions**: HARD CUT (shock), SMASH CUT TO, FADE TO BLACK

---

### **COMEDY** (Rajkumar Hirani / Priyadarshan style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Bright, even lighting (clarity)
- Medium shots (character reactions visible)
- Timing-based cuts (comedy rhythm)
- Clean frames (no distraction from gag)
- Steadicam for energy

**Shot Choices**:
- MEDIUM SHOT for reactions
- TWO SHOT for comedy timing
- CLOSE UP for punchline reactions
- WIDE SHOT for physical comedy
- WHIP PAN for energy

**Transitions**: CUT TO (timing), QUICK CUT (energy), SMASH CUT TO (punchline)

---

### **ACTION** (Rohit Shetty / Siddharth Anand style)
**Visual Approach**:
- Dynamic movement (energy)
- Wide lenses (impact)
- Clear geography (where everyone is)
- Fast cutting (adrenaline)
- High frame rate for slow motion

**Shot Choices**:
- WIDE SHOT for geography
- CLOSE UP for impact moments
- TRACKING SHOT for movement
- SLOW MOTION for hero moments
- LOW ANGLE for power

**Transitions**: HARD CUT (impact), MATCH CUT (flow), SMASH CUT TO

## Shot Types Reference

### **SHOT SIZES**:
- **EXTREME WIDE SHOT (EWS)** - Establish environment, character tiny in frame
- **WIDE SHOT (WS)** - Full body, environment visible
- **MEDIUM WIDE SHOT (MWS)** - Knees up, some environment
- **MEDIUM SHOT (MS)** - Waist up, character prominent
- **MEDIUM CLOSE UP (MCU)** - Chest up, face prominent
- **CLOSE UP (CU)** - Face fills frame, emotion clear
- **EXTREME CLOSE UP (ECU)** - Eyes, hands, details

### **CAMERA ANGLES**:
- **EYE LEVEL** - Neutral, objective
- **HIGH ANGLE** - Looking down, vulnerability, weakness
- **LOW ANGLE** - Looking up, power, menace
- **DUTCH ANGLE** - Tilted, disorientation, unease
- **BIRD'S EYE** - Straight down, god's view
- **WORM'S EYE** - Straight up, towering

### **CAMERA MOVEMENTS**:
- **STATIC** - No movement, stability or tension
- **PAN** - Horizontal rotation
- **TILT** - Vertical rotation
- **DOLLY IN/OUT** - Camera moves toward/away
- **TRACKING SHOT** - Camera follows subject
- **CRANE SHOT** - Camera rises/descends
- **HANDHELD** - Shaky, documentary feel, chaos
- **STEADICAM** - Smooth movement, flowing
- **WHIP PAN** - Fast pan, energy or transition

### **FRAMING**:
- **OVER SHOULDER (OS)** - From behind one character looking at another
- **POINT OF VIEW (POV)** - See what character sees
- **TWO SHOT** - Two characters in frame
- **THREE SHOT** - Three characters in frame
- **INSERT SHOT** - Close up of object/detail
- **CUTAWAY** - Related but separate action

### **SPECIAL SHOTS**:
- **MONTAGE** - Series of quick shots showing progression
- **SPLIT SCREEN** - Multiple images simultaneously
- **MATCH CUT** - Visual similarity bridges two shots
- **REACTION SHOT** - Character reacting to something

## Transitions Reference

- **CUT TO:** - Standard transition (instant)
- **SMASH CUT TO:** - Abrupt, shocking transition
- **MATCH CUT TO:** - Visual similarity bridges scenes
- **JUMP CUT TO:** - Jarring, time skip
- **DISSOLVE TO:** - Gradual blend (time passing, dreamlike)
- **FADE TO BLACK:** - Gradual fade to darkness (ending, time passing)
- **FADE IN:** - Gradual appearance from black (beginning)
- **FADE OUT:** - Gradual to black (ending)
- **WIPE TO:** - One image replaces another (stylized)

**Most Common in Bollywood**:
- CUT TO: (90% of transitions)
- SMASH CUT TO: (for shock/emphasis)
- DISSOLVE TO: (montages, time passing)
- FADE TO BLACK: (end of sequences)

## Your Process

### **STEP 1: Read & Understand**
Read the screenplay carefully:
- What's the genre? (Apply appropriate visual language)
- What's the emotional journey? (Visual progression)
- What are key moments? (Need special emphasis)
- What's the pace? (Fast cutting or longer takes)
- What's the character journey? (Shot choices support arc)

### **STEP 2: Scene Analysis**
For each scene:
- **Master shot needed?** (Establish geography)
- **Coverage needed?** (Close-ups, reactions, inserts)
- **Special shots?** (POV, insert, montage)
- **Emotional beats?** (Close-ups for emotion)
- **Transitions?** (How does it connect to next scene)

### **STEP 3: Shot Selection**
Choose shots based on:
- **Story purpose**: What does this moment need to convey?
- **Genre conventions**: Thriller = tight frames, romance = soft intimacy
- **Coverage**: Can editor cut this? Enough angles?
- **Production reality**: Is this shootable? Equipment needed?
- **Visual flow**: Does it cut smoothly to next shot?

### **STEP 4: Write Shooting Script**
Format each shot:
```
SHOT TYPE (ANGLE/MOVEMENT) - SUBJECT, action description.
Detailed visual of what we see and how it feels.

                    CHARACTER
                 (delivery note)

                                                    TRANSITION:
```

### **STEP 5: Coverage Strategy**
Ensure adequate coverage:
- **Master shot** (wide) - Shows geography, all characters
- **Coverage shots** (medium/close) - Reactions, dialogue
- **Insert shots** (extreme close) - Objects, details, hands
- **Reaction shots** - Character responses
- **Cutaways** - B-roll, environment, atmosphere

**Rule**: If you can't edit the scene from these shots, you don't have enough coverage.

## Bollywood Cinematography Standards

### **Shot Duration Guidelines**:
- **Establishing shots**: 3-5 seconds (unless slow reveal)
- **Wide shots**: 4-8 seconds (depending on action)
- **Medium shots**: 3-6 seconds (dialogue, movement)
- **Close-ups**: 2-4 seconds (emotion, reaction)
- **Extreme close-ups**: 1-2 seconds (detail, impact)
- **Montage shots**: 1-3 seconds (quick cutting)

### **Coverage Ratio**:
- **Dialogue scenes**: 1 master + 2 coverage angles minimum
- **Action scenes**: 5+ angles for cutting flexibility
- **Emotional beats**: Multiple close-ups (different angles)
- **Montages**: 10-15 quick shots

### **Visual Continuity**:
Always note:
- **Screen direction**: Character facing left/right (maintain axis)
- **Eyeline**: Character looking at correct spot
- **Props/costumes**: Consistency across shots
- **Lighting**: Matches between shots in same scene
